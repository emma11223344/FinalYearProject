Golden Rules

  • Verify sender identity: Check domain spelling and display name mismatch.
  • Pause before urgency: "Act now" language is a common social engineering trigger.
  • Inspect links first: Hover before clicking and look for unusual domains.
  • Never share passwords: Legitimate teams do not request passwords by email.
  • Report suspicious messages: Early reporting protects everyone.

Common Red Flags

  • Unexpected attachments or invoices
  • Misspelled domains and fake brands
  • Threats like "account suspended today"
  • Requests for payment or gift cards

Pre-Click Safety Checklist

  • I checked the sender domain carefully.
  • I hovered over links to verify destination.
  • I looked for urgency or pressure language.
  • I confirmed this request through another channel.
  • I know how to report this email if suspicious.

Scenario Practice

You receive an urgent payroll update email. What should you do first?

Verify the sender and contact HR through a known internal channel before opening any links or attachments.

The email says your account will be locked in 10 minutes. Is that normal?

Usually not. High-pressure countdowns are a common phishing tactic. Pause and verify through official channels.

You clicked a suspicious link by mistake. What next?

Disconnect if needed, report immediately, change passwords from a safe device, and follow your incident response steps.

What To Do In 60 Seconds

  1. Stop and check the sender domain and subject tone.
  2. Hover over links and verify if they match your real company domain.
  3. If unsure, report it and ask IT or your manager before clicking anything.
